problem

for years, barkbox held the same assumptions about potential customers, how they perceived our products, and how they wanted to shop for them. neither those assumptions—nor the site itself—have ever been tested and the company's digital experience has remained largely unchanged for half a decade.

role

as the lead designer for a small growth product squad—pm, two devs, and myself—i was tasked with conducting & synthesizing research, helping scope and plan roadmaps and goals, ideating and testing product features, and assisting with front-end.

overview

we conducted problem-space research to gather qualitative insights from dog parents to better understand potential customers and how they like to shop for their dogs and what is most important when doing so.

interviews

we interviewed 16 dogs owners—screened to meet our requirements—for one hour, starting with general questions about their shopping habits, what they look for, what they value, etc., and ending with observing them navigate through the current purchase experience and voice aloud any questions, comments, or concerns.

in a nutshell, we learned most of our assumptions were wrong

insights

in a nutshell, we learned most of our assumptions were wrong— most notably our assumption that people knew what they were signing up for. values deemed important by most interviewees—like treats made in the usa and exclusive, themed toys—also went unnoticed. no wonder people were dropping like flies once they reached checkout.

cx journey

we learned that—just like dogs—dog parents come in all shapes and sizes. it's difficult to give each one exactly what they want at each step of the way, but we can hone in on the experience that empowers them to make the best decision for themselves, their dog, and their family.

understanding the entire cx journey, not just as it relates to our squad

roadmap

01 — consideration

02 — customization

03 — checkout

consideration

the first phase of a customer's journey as it pertains to the growth squad. we're responsible for everything that happens from the moment someone hits the homepage until they've clicked the 'buy now' button or—should we dare even say—they leave.

goals

  • increase engagement with educational content before clicking into the purchase funnel.

  • reduce the number of support inquiries made to the 'happy team' by increasing transparency and education.

customization

we learned customization—or at least the perception of it—was hugely important for dog parents. from plan length to toy material to protein preferences, every decision that goes into making a pup's box is an important one.

goals

  • better illustrate the value of longer term commitment and decrease number of 1-month sign-ups to churn.

  • increase first box nps by setting better expectations for what comes in a barkbox.

checkout

ah, the infamous checkout. the metric suits care most about. the hardest of all numbers to 'move the needle' on. lucky for me, ours was pretty bad, so things could only get better, right?

goals

  • reduce the number of user-input errors at checkout by providing a cleaner, more accessible checkout process.

  • increase purchase conversion by making checkout quick and easy and reiterating product values.

ideation

now that we knew what dog parents were looking for and had a better understanding of how they perceived our current experience, it was time to explore the plethora of ways we might approach learning about someone's dog(s).

testing

throughout the ideation process, we used a combination of wireframe, invision, and live prototypes to get our ideas in front of users and help refine our decisions.

iteration

feedback from our problem-space interviews was immensely helpful and our user testing was no different. by fine-tuning the ui and asking the right questions, we were able to ship improvements that had a positive impact on the experience.

metrics

overall, our refresh of the barkbox purchase experience was a major success. through countless iterations and a/b tests at all stages of the experience, we were able to increase purchase conversion from top-of-funnel by nearly 6%.

takeaways

some goals were axed and others not met at the expense of other stakeholder decisions, but needless to say we were happy with our results.

  • increase engagement with educational content before clicking into the purchase funnel.

  • reduce the number of support inquiries made to the 'happy team' by increasing transparency and education.

  • better illustrate the value of longer term commitment and decrease number of 1-month sign-ups to churn.

  • increase first box nps by setting better expectations for what comes in a barkbox.

  • reduce the number of user-input errors at checkout by providing a cleaner, more accessible checkout process.

  • increase purchase conversion by making checkout quick and easy and reiterating product values.
